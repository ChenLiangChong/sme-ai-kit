# PMM Gate 2: Positioning Development

Category design, positioning statements, and competitive framing using April Dunford methodology.

---

## Positioning Development

Develop positioning using April Dunford methodology:

1. List competitive alternatives (direct, adjacent, status quo)
2. Isolate unique attributes (features only you have)
3. Map attributes to customer value (why it matters)
4. Define best-fit customers (who cares most)
5. Choose market category (head-to-head, niche, new category)
6. Layer on relevant trends (timing justification)
7. Test with 10+ customer interviews
8. **Validation:** 7+ customers describe value unprompted

### Positioning Statement Template

```
FOR [target customer]
WHO [statement of need]
THE [product] IS A [category]
THAT [key benefit]
UNLIKE [competitive alternative]
OUR PRODUCT [primary differentiation]
```

### Value Proposition Formula

Template: `[Product] helps [Target Customer] [Achieve Goal] by [Unique Approach]`

Example: "Acme helps mid-market teams ship 2x faster by automating project workflows with AI"

## Category Strategy

### Option 1: Head-to-Head

- **When**: Strong product, known category, clear advantages over incumbent
- **Risk**: Incumbent has brand recognition, resources, and installed base
- **Message**: "Better [category] because [differentiation]"
- **Proof needed**: Direct comparison wins, benchmark data, switching stories
- **Example**: "A CRM that's actually easy to use" (vs. Salesforce)

### Option 2: Niche Down

- **When**: Specific segment is underserved, you have deep domain expertise
- **Risk**: Smaller addressable market, ceiling on growth
- **Message**: "[Category] built specifically for [segment]"
- **Proof needed**: Deep domain knowledge, segment-specific features, testimonials
- **Example**: "Project management for construction teams" (vs. generic PM tools)

### Option 3: Create New Category

- **When**: Truly novel approach, no direct comparison exists
- **Risk**: Market education cost, longer sales cycle, category confusion
- **Message**: "[New category name] — a new way to [outcome]"
- **Proof needed**: Thought leadership, market trend data, early adopter success
- **Example**: "Revenue Intelligence" (Gong creating a new category)

### Category Decision Matrix

| Factor | Head-to-Head | Niche Down | New Category |
|--------|-------------|------------|--------------|
| Market education needed | Low | Low | High |
| Sales cycle | Short | Short | Long |
| Competition intensity | High | Low | None (initially) |
| Growth ceiling | High | Medium | Very High |
| Marketing budget needed | High | Medium | Very High |
| Best for stage | Growth | Early | Well-funded |

## Messaging Hierarchy

| Level | Content | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Headline | 5-7 words | "Ship faster with AI automation" |
| Subhead | 1 sentence | "Automate workflows so teams focus on what matters" |
| Benefits | 3-4 bullets | Speed, quality, collaboration, cost |
| Features | Supporting evidence | AI automation → 10 hrs/week saved |
| Proof | Social proof | Customer logos, stats, case studies |

## Positioning Validation

### Interview Protocol (10+ customers)

1. "How would you describe [product] to a colleague?"
2. "What were you using before? What was missing?"
3. "What's the #1 thing [product] does that nothing else does?"
4. "If [product] disappeared tomorrow, what would you use instead?"
5. "What category would you put [product] in?"
6. "What made you choose [product] over alternatives?"
7. "What would you tell someone who's considering [competitor]?"

### Success Criteria

| Signal | Threshold | Status |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Customers describe value unprompted | 7+ out of 10 | Required |
| Consistent language across interviews | Shared vocabulary emerges | Required |
| Clear differentiation from alternatives | Unique attributes cited | Required |
| Category resonates without explanation | No confusion about what you are | Required |
| "I'd be upset if this disappeared" | 40%+ (Sean Ellis test) | Strong signal |

### Positioning Audit Checklist

- [ ] Competitive alternatives mapped (all 3 tiers)
- [ ] Unique attributes documented (features only you have)
- [ ] Attributes → customer value mapped
- [ ] Best-fit customer segment defined
- [ ] Market category chosen (with rationale)
- [ ] Trend alignment identified
- [ ] 10+ customer validation interviews completed
- [ ] Positioning statement written and approved
- [ ] All marketing assets updated to reflect positioning

## Competitive Positioning Map

### How to Build

1. Choose 2 axes that matter most to your best-fit customers
2. Plot yourself and all competitors on the map
3. Identify open space (underserved quadrant)
4. Validate that the open space has actual demand
5. Position yourself in the high-value open space

### Template

```
                    [Axis 1: High]
                         |
    [Competitor A]       |       [YOU]
                         |
  [Axis 2: Low] --------+-------- [Axis 2: High]
                         |
    [Competitor B]       |    [Competitor C]
                         |
                    [Axis 1: Low]
```

### Common Positioning Axes

| Industry | Axis 1 | Axis 2 |
|----------|--------|--------|
| SaaS | Ease of use ↔ Power | SMB ↔ Enterprise |
| Legal Tech | Accuracy ↔ Speed | Individual ↔ Firm |
| Marketing | Automation ↔ Control | Channel-specific ↔ Omnichannel |
| Analytics | Real-time ↔ Deep analysis | Self-serve ↔ Managed |

## Proactive Triggers

- **No documented positioning** → Without clear positioning, all marketing is guesswork.
- **Competitor repositioning** → Market shift detected. Review your positioning.
- **New market entry** → Positioning may not transfer across markets.
- **Product pivot** → Positioning must evolve with the product.
